<i>Do you want to evaluate the completeness of your data and evidence and find out if you are ready to explore the development of quantitative Adverse Outcome Pathway (qAOP) models for your problem of interest?</i>
<br>
<br>
I have put down a list of essential questions to guide anyone interested in qAOPs. This follows my experience and knowledge accumulated during the past years. This is also a result of being approached by experimentalists and other researchers for such types of assessments. 
<br>
<br>
However, I see it as a starting point because it shall be case by case and rather adapted. Even more questions will arise than the ones listed below. 
<br>
2. <b>Hypothesis</b>. What is the question you want to answer to? What are you aiming to quantify? What is possible to be achieved? This is very much linked to the type of output you want the model to give, e.g., a point of departure, a risk classification, or a causal effect estimate, to name a few.
3. <b>Data</b>. What kind of data do you have or are going to obtain? E.g., concentration-responses and time-responses in full range, EC/IC or NOAEL/LOAEL types of values. Also, is the dataset complete or does it contain missing data points? This is important if an imputation method is necessary to be considered or how and if to quantify the uncertainty of the identified gaps.
4. <b>Qualitative AOPs</b>. What kind of AOP-related knowledge do you have? E.g., a list of non-ordered biological events, linear AOPs, AOP networks. Do those biological events cover MIEs (receptors oriented), earlier KEs at the cellular level, tissue level, biomarkers?
5. <b>Methodology</b>. How are the data measured? E.g., literature review, conducted experiments based on functional assays or qPCR, RNAseq, etc. This will help to understand the units of the responses, i.e., counts, % out of control, and decide on the definitions of the so-called tipping points. Even if it is in terms of probabilities, those probabilities should express something, e.g., the probability of that specific % out of control response to be seen at X concentration and Y time point.
6. <b>Causality</b>. This aspect is crucial when it comes to the qAOP, and it has to be proven and justified before claiming that a model is a qAOP. The question is if the directed acyclic graph or the qualitative AOP or the initial hypothesis was evaluated for causality, e.g., at least based on the modified Bradford Hill criteria. Otherwise, the model becomes a QSAR be it a regression or classification problem. It shouldn't be about identifying biological effects related to a mechanism that describes an endpoint or an adverse effect. It should be about a sequential series of events linked causally.
7. <b>Key event relationships</b>. What type of biological processes are aimed to be studied and modelled mathematically? E.g., feedback loops, adaptive responses, injury, a mix of direct and indirect linked events, or expressing one direction. Also, are these key events unique for the AOP / endpoint?
8. <b>Extrapolation to humans</b>. This is another crucial aspect for both regulatory purposes as well as for the acceptance of the alternative methods overall (thinking of drug discovery and not necessarily about the chemical industry like cosmetics where new approach methodologies must be used according to the EU law). Are there any exposure data, e.g., for validation purposes, and available PBPK models and IVIVE information?  

A last advice, document and report your workflow about the computational modelling, <i>in vitro</i> experiments, causal construction as detailed as possible. There is no reporting guideline at the moment, but you can see it as a great opportunity to shape and contribute to a future one.
<br>
<br>
Wish you good luck and most importantly enjoy whatever problem you aim to solve! 😊
